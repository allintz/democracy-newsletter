The Secret Ballot Wasn't Always Secret: What We Learned About Vote Buying and Intimidation

Reading time: 5 minutes

Epistemic status: High confidence on historical facts (well-documented in academic literature), moderate confidence on exact mechanisms that changed voter behavior (some inference from available data).

---

If you're building intuitions about election subversion, here's a foundational concept worth understanding: the secret ballot is actually a pretty recent innovation in American democracy. And the story of why we adopted it teaches us something crucial about institutional design.

For most of the 1800s, voting in America was completely public. I don't mean public in the sense of "your voting record is available online." I mean you walked up to a table, announced your choices out loud to election officials, or handed them a colored ballot that made your vote visible to everyone watching.

This wasn't a bug. It was the design.

---

How Vote Buying Actually Worked

Let me paint a picture of what elections looked like in the 1880s.

Political parties printed their own ballots—often on distinctively colored paper. A Republican ballot might be white, a Democratic ballot tan. You'd pick up your party's ballot (often from party workers standing right outside the polling place) and deposit it in the ballot box, where everyone could see which colored paper you'd chosen (MIT Press: "The Australian Ballot").

This created a simple enforcement mechanism for vote buying: "I'll give you $5 to vote Republican" was verifiable. The buyer could literally watch you take the Republican ballot and deposit it. No secret ballot meant no way to take the money and vote differently.

According to historian Jac Heckelman's research analyzing voting patterns before and after secret ballot adoption, this system was pervasive. In some urban areas, vote buying was estimated to affect 15-20% of voters in competitive races (Heckelman, "The Effect of the Secret Ballot on Voter Turnout Rates," Public Choice, 1995).

The open ballot also enabled intimidation. Employers could watch their workers vote. Landlords could monitor tenants. In the post-Reconstruction South, this became a tool for suppressing Black voter participation—though after the 1890s, other more explicit disenfranchisement methods became dominant (Alexander Keyssar, "The Right to Vote," pp. 111-116).

---

The Australian Solution

The fix came from, of all places, Australia. Starting in 1856, Australian colonies began implementing what became known as the "Australian ballot" system. The reforms had several components:

  • Government-printed ballots instead of party-printed ones
  • Secret voting booths where no one could watch your choice
  • Standardized ballot format listing all candidates
  • Distribution at the polling place by election officials, not party workers

    • Massachusetts adopted this system in 1888, becoming the first U.S. state to do so. By 1896, 90% of states had implemented some version of the Australian ballot (Eldon Cobb Evans, "A History of the Australian Ballot System in the United States," University of Chicago Press, 1917).

    The speed of adoption is actually remarkable. In just eight years, a fundamental redesign of how Americans voted became nearly universal across states.

    Why so fast?

    ---

    What Changed (And What We Can Learn)

    The answer tells us something important about institutional reform: the secret ballot succeeded because it solved a problem that multiple political factions cared about, even if for different reasons.

    Good-government reformers wanted to reduce corruption. But political parties themselves sometimes supported it—when a party couldn't afford to compete in vote buying, the secret ballot leveled the playing field. Businesses that were tired of being shaken down to fund political machines also backed reform (Peter Argersinger, "A Place on the Ballot," University of Iowa Press, 1992, pp. 47-68).

    The effects were measurable. Heckelman's analysis found that after secret ballot adoption:

  • Voter turnout dropped by approximately 7-12% on average
  • The decline was larger in states with more competitive elections (where vote buying had been more prevalent)
  • Split-ticket voting (voting for different parties for different offices) increased significantly

    • That first point might seem counterintuitive—isn't less turnout bad? But here's the reasoning: the turnout drop likely represented the elimination of purchased votes and coerced votes. Meanwhile, genuine voters now had more freedom to vote their actual preferences, which we can infer from the increase in split-ticket voting.

    This is a clear example of how procedural changes can reduce a specific attack vector on election integrity. Before secret ballots, buying votes was a straightforward transaction. After secret ballots, it became nearly impossible to verify compliance—why pay for something you can't confirm?

    ---

    The Vulnerability That Remains

    Here's where this connects to modern election subversion concerns: the secret ballot solved vote buying at the individual level, but it didn't address institutional manipulation—changing who counts votes, how they're counted, or whether they're counted at all.

    The mechanisms matter. The Australian ballot works because:

  • Physical privacy prevents observation during voting
  • Standardized process makes verification impossible
  • Universal adoption eliminates compliant alternatives

    • For an institutional safeguard to work against subversion attempts, it needs similar characteristics. It needs to be:

  • Hard to circumvent through alternative mechanisms
  • Checkable by multiple parties
  • Adopted broadly enough that workarounds don't exist

    • This is why, for example, modern concerns focus on things like:

  • Partisan control of certification (no competing verification)
  • Rejection of valid votes (circumvents individual choice)
  • Interference with counting (bypasses the voter entirely)

    • The secret ballot protected the individual voter's choice. But if officials can simply refuse to count or certify those choices, the protection is incomplete.

    ---

    Bottom Line

    The secret ballot teaches us three key lessons for thinking about election integrity:

    First: Institutional design can eliminate specific attack vectors. Once secret ballots were adopted, traditional vote buying became functionally impossible. The mechanism worked because it made verification impossible for the buyer.

    Second: Successful reforms often succeed when they align interests across political factions. The secret ballot won adoption not purely through good-government advocacy, but because various groups saw advantages.

    Third: Solving one level of the problem doesn't solve all levels. The secret ballot protected individual vote choice but not the counting, certification, and institutional machinery that sits between the voter and the final result. That's where attention shifted—and where it remains today.

    Understanding this history helps us think more clearly about modern vulnerabilities. We're not dealing with vote buying at polling places anymore. We're dealing with pressure on the institutional machinery that comes after votes are cast. The attack surface has moved, which means the safeguards need to address different mechanisms.
