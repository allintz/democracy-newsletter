The Mechanics of Election Certification: Where Democracy's Paperwork Becomes Its Frontline

Epistemic status: High confidence on legal mechanics and documented 2020 incidents; medium confidence on predicting future vulnerabilities.

Here's something I find fascinating about election subversion: the most vulnerable moment isn't when people vote. It's during the weeks afterward, when officials sit in rooms with paper and computers, adding up numbers and signing certificates.

This boring administrative process—election certification—became the target of intense pressure campaigns in 2020. And it's worth understanding in detail, because it reveals both how democratic institutions held and where they remain fragile.

What Actually Happens During Certification

Let me walk you through the process, because it's more complicated (and more interesting) than most people realize.

First, certification happens at three levels: county, state, and federal. Each level depends on the one before it.

County level: After polls close, local election boards (usually 3-5 members) meet to certify their county's results. They review canvass reports, reconcile vote totals, and vote to certify. This typically happens within 1-2 weeks of Election Day. According to the National Association of Counties, over 8,000 local jurisdictions conduct elections in the US, meaning thousands of these small board meetings happen after every election.

State level: The chief election official (usually the Secretary of State) then certifies the statewide results, typically by a legal deadline around 3-4 weeks after the election. They issue a "certificate of ascertainment" listing the winning candidates and, for presidential elections, the electors they've won.

Federal level: For presidential elections only, the Electoral College meets in mid-December (date set by federal law), then Congress counts electoral votes on January 6th.

Here's the key thing: at each level, certification is supposed to be ministerial, not discretionary. Officials are meant to verify that proper procedures were followed, then certify the results—not investigate fraud claims or substitute their judgment for voters'.

But what happens when officials refuse to do their ministerial duty?

The 2020 Wayne County Near-Miss

On November 17, 2020, something unprecedented almost happened in Michigan's largest county.

The Wayne County Board of Canvassers—a four-member body with two Republicans and two Democrats—met to certify Detroit's election results. Biden had won the county by over 330,000 votes, according to official county records.

The two Republican members, Monica Palmer and William Hartmann, initially voted against certification. Their stated reason was discrepancies in poll books in Detroit precincts—routine clerical issues that election experts noted occur in every election and don't indicate fraud.

The vote deadlocked 2-2. Certification failed.

Think about what this meant: Wayne County contains Detroit, which is 40% of Michigan's population. If Wayne County didn't certify, Michigan couldn't certify. If Michigan couldn't certify by the federal deadline (six days before the Electoral College meets), its electoral votes might be thrown into legal chaos.

After intense public pressure during the Zoom meeting—including testimony from Detroit residents—Palmer and Hartmann reversed their votes. Certification passed. Then, hours later, they attempted to rescind their votes in signed affidavits, though this had no legal effect.

But here's what keeps me up at night: it almost worked. Two local officials nearly derailed certification for a county of 1.7 million people based on routine clerical discrepancies.

The Pattern Across States

Wayne County wasn't unique. Similar pressure campaigns played out across battleground states, targeting the often-obscure officials who certify elections.

Arizona: In Cochise County, two Republican supervisors initially refused to certify the November 2022 election by the legal deadline, citing concerns about voting equipment certification. A court ordered them to certify. Had they succeeded in blocking certification, it could have affected the statewide results in races decided by narrow margins.

New Mexico: In Otero County, the county commission refused to certify the June 2022 primary election, citing distrust in Dominion voting machines despite no evidence of problems. The state Supreme Court ordered certification.

Pennsylvania: In 2020, Republican state legislators pushed to have the legislature appoint electors regardless of the popular vote. This didn't succeed, but the attempt revealed the theory they were working with: that legislatures could substitute their judgment for voters'.

The common thread? Officials at various levels testing whether they could simply refuse to certify results they didn't like.

Why This Matters More Than Fraud Claims

I want to be clear about something: there's an important difference between these certification battles and claims of voter fraud.

Voter fraud claims in 2020 were comprehensively investigated and rejected by over 60 courts, often by Trump-appointed judges. The fraud simply didn't happen at scale.

But the certification battles? Those were real institutional stress tests. Officials actually did refuse to certify. Boards actually did deadlock. Courts actually did have to intervene to force basic administrative duties.

In other words: you don't need to prove voter fraud to subvert an election. You just need enough officials at enough levels to refuse to do their jobs.

This is a different kind of vulnerability than most people imagine. It's not about hackers or fake ballots. It's about exploiting the fact that our system depends on thousands of local officials performing ministerial duties, and there aren't always clear legal consequences for refusing.

The Legal Gray Zones

Here's where things get complicated. What happens when a certification board refuses to certify?

The answer varies by state, and the law is surprisingly unclear in many places. A UCLA Law review analysis found that while certification is ministerial in theory, enforcement mechanisms vary wildly:

  • Some states have clear mandamus procedures (where courts can order officials to perform their duties)
  • Some have criminal penalties for refusal to certify
  • Some have... nothing clearly specified

    • In Wayne County, there was no immediate legal remedy to force certification. It worked because the officials reversed themselves under public pressure. But what if they hadn't?

    This ambiguity is dangerous. It means bad actors can test the system, see how far they can push, and face minimal consequences if they back down.

    What Changed After 2020

    The good news: some states have since tightened their laws.

    Michigan clarified in 2021 that certification is ministerial and courts can compel it. Nevada passed similar reforms. Other states have explored making refusal to certify a criminal offense.

    But many states haven't acted. And Congress, despite passing the Electoral Count Reform Act in 2022 to clarify federal-level procedures, didn't address state and county certification.

    The result is a patchwork where some jurisdictions have clear rules and others remain vulnerable.

    Looking Ahead to 2026 and 2028

    So what should we watch for in upcoming elections?

    First, pay attention to who controls county boards of canvassers and state boards of elections. These used to be boring administrative posts. They're now positions of significant power.

    Second, watch for continued testing of boundaries. After 2020, we saw more certification resistance in 2022 (Cochise County, Otero County). This suggests some officials learned that refusing to certify generates attention and political support in certain circles, even if it ultimately fails.

    Third, recognize that court intervention worked so far, but courts are slow. If a county refuses to certify and litigation takes weeks, you might hit federal deadlines before getting resolution. That's a genuine vulnerability.

    The certification process is meant to be boring. The fact that it's become contested is itself a warning sign.

    Bottom Line: Relevance for Democracy Projects

    If you're thinking about starting a project in the democracy space, the certification vulnerability suggests several angles:

    1. Legal clarity projects: Many states still have ambiguous laws about certification. Model legislation making certification clearly ministerial, with fast judicial review and penalties for refusal, could be valuable. The States United Democracy Center does some of this work but there's room for more state-specific analysis.

    2. Election official support: Local election officials faced enormous pressure in 2020-2022. Organizations that provide legal support, security resources, and community backing for officials performing their ministerial duties serve a real need. The Committee of Seventy in Philadelphia provides a model.

    3. Public education: Most people don't understand the certification process until it becomes controversial. Educational campaigns explaining how certification works—and why it must be ministerial—could build public support for officials who follow the law.

    4. Monitoring and rapid response: A network that tracks certification meetings, identifies potential refusals early, and mobilizes legal and public pressure quickly could prevent crises. This is time-sensitive work that needs infrastructure.

    The key insight is this: election subversion doesn't require a conspiracy or sophisticated fraud. It just requires enough boring administrative meetings where officials refuse to sign forms they're legally obligated to sign. That's a surprisingly mundane threat. But mundane doesn't mean manageable—it means we need to fix these administrative vulnerabilities before the next close election tests them again.
